I've spent the last 20 years building, fixing, and optimizing supply chains - from factory floors in China to distribution centers in the US, from ERP implementations to warehouse robotics. And for the last two years, I've been deep in the world of AI — not just reading about it, but building with it.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: most supply chains aren't ready for AI.

Not because the technology isn't there. It is. The models are powerful, the tools are maturing, and the cost of inference is plummeting.

The problem is something far more fundamental - and it's the same problem I've seen kill every major technology wave in supply chain over the last two decades.

## The Pattern That Keeps Repeating

When ERP systems first arrived, companies rushed to implement SAP, Oracle, JDA. The technology was transformative. But the implementations? Brutal. Over budget, over timeline, and often delivering a fraction of the promised value.

Why? Because companies tried to automate broken processes. They took their existing chaos and digitized it.

The same thing happened with IoT. With blockchain. With "digital twins." Each time, the technology worked in demos but stumbled in production because the underlying foundation wasn't solid.

AI is following the exact same pattern.

## The Four Foundations Most Companies Are Missing

After working with dozens of supply chain operations, I've identified four critical foundations that determine whether AI will deliver real value or become another expensive science project:

**1. Data Architecture**

AI doesn't need perfect data. But it does need connected data. Most supply chains have their data trapped in silos — one system for procurement, another for inventory, another for transportation, and none of them talking to each other in real time.

Before you write a single AI prompt, ask yourself: Can I get a unified view of my supply chain data within 15 minutes? If the answer is no, that's your first problem to solve.

**2. Process Maturity**

AI amplifies what already exists. If your demand planning process is a monthly spreadsheet exercise driven by gut feel, AI won't magically fix it. It'll just give you faster gut feel with a veneer of sophistication.

You need standardized, documented processes with clear inputs, outputs, and decision points. Not perfect processes — but defined ones that AI can learn from and improve.

**3. Decision Framework**

Here's what most AI vendors won't tell you: the hard part isn't generating AI recommendations. It's deciding what to do with them.

Which decisions should be fully automated? Which need human review? What's the escalation path when AI confidence is low? Without a clear decision framework, you'll end up with a team that either blindly follows AI suggestions or ignores them entirely.

**4. Change Management**

The warehouse manager who's been doing demand planning for 15 years isn't going to trust a black box. And honestly, they shouldn't — at least not immediately.

You need a deliberate plan for how humans and AI will work together. Not "AI replaces people" (it won't, not in supply chain, not for a long time) but "AI augments people" in specific, measurable ways.

## What's Actually Working Right Now

Despite the hype, there are real, practical AI applications delivering value in supply chains today:

- **Demand Sensing:** Using AI to incorporate real-time signals (weather, social media, economic indicators) into short-term demand forecasts. Not replacing traditional forecasting, but augmenting it with signals humans can't process at scale.
- **Exception Management:** Instead of humans reviewing thousands of alerts daily, AI triages and prioritizes exceptions, presenting only the ones that truly need human judgment.
- **Document Processing:** Purchase orders, invoices, shipping documents — AI can extract, validate, and route these with accuracy that matches or exceeds manual processing.
- **Supplier Risk Monitoring:** Continuously scanning news, financial data, and operational signals to flag potential supplier disruptions before they hit your supply chain.

Notice what's NOT on this list: fully autonomous supply chain planning, AI-driven strategic sourcing negotiations, or self-optimizing logistics networks. Those are coming, but they're not ready for production today, regardless of what vendor demos suggest.

## The Real Competitive Advantage

Here's what I believe most deeply: the companies that will win with AI in supply chain aren't the ones with the biggest AI budgets or the most sophisticated models.

They're the ones that do the boring work first.

Clean your data. Standardize your processes. Build your decision frameworks. Prepare your people.

Then, when you deploy AI, it will have a solid foundation to build on. And the results will be transformative — not because the AI is magical, but because it's amplifying a well-designed system.
